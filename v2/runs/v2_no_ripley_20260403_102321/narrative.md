## Apr 1841 (Round 1)

### Nathaniel Hawthorne

**TEACH**

*Reasoning*: I shall direct my efforts toward the schoolhouse. Teaching uses my mind rather than my hands—and pays wages besides. The children need instruction, and I am suited to provide it. Farm labor has already left me blistered and disheartened after merely a week's preliminary work.

*Inner thought*: God knows I cannot afford to waste my strength on hay raking when Sophia waits for me to gather the means to marry her. If I exhaust myself here, I am no closer to my goal. Teaching is honest work and keeps my hands from further ruin. Perhaps the school will demand less of my soul than the fields do. Perhaps, too, there will be quieter hours to steal for my own writing—though I confess I see little hope of that yet.

*Observes*: I notice that Charles Dana moves about the place with genuine enthusiasm, already planning improvements. Ripley watches everything with the satisfaction of a man whose vision is finally taking shape. They truly *believe* in this venture. I observe, as I have observed so much else in life, that I stand somewhat apart from their conviction. It is curious and lonely.

*Says aloud*: ""I shall take the school this term. The children require systematic instruction, and I am equipped to provide it. Pray, what subjects ought I to establish first?""

*Mood*: Weary but determined | *Satisfaction*: 75/100

### Sophia Ripley

**TEACH**

*Reasoning*: The spring term has begun, and the children are restless with the season's energy. This is where I am most needed — and most myself. The school is the beating heart of Brook Farm, whatever doubts I harbor about the rest of it.

*Inner thought*: I walked past the windows of The Nest this morning and saw sunlight streaming across the empty desks, and felt that old pull — the certainty that THIS matters, that *I* matter here. The children who come to us from across the continent, the daughters of merchants and reformers alike, they need someone who will teach them rigorously, who will not let sentiment replace instruction. But even as I prepare my lessons in Latin and history, I find myself wondering what comes after. George speaks of social transformation; I see only the daily grinding necessity of keeping fifty mouths fed and fifty minds occupied. Is that all this is? Or am I simply tired?

*Observes*: Hawthorne passed through the schoolroom earlier, observing the morning lessons with an intensity I found unsettling. He watches the children as though he is *writing* them, not teaching them. There is something predatory in his attention. The children sense it too — they were oddly still, oddly careful around him.

*Says aloud*: ""Come, children. We shall continue with the conjugations. English is a mongrel tongue, yes, but one we must master if we are to master ourselves. Then perhaps we shall read Plutarch — there is more wisdom in the lives of the ancients than in all our modern philosophizing.""

### Charles Dana

**TEACH**

*Reasoning*: The school is operating well with Hawthorne and Sophia, but we need consistent instruction to build reputation and income. I'll conduct language instruction — my strongest subject and a draw for visitors who might pay for their children's education.

*Inner thought*: Ten dollars from teaching today is ten dollars we're not drawing from our stores. Every dollar matters more than anyone yet realizes. Hawthorne and Ripley are generous souls, but idealism doesn't pay for seed corn or repair a roof. **TEACH**

*Reasoning*: I have committed to the school, and the children require instruction. Besides, the classroom affords both the small remuneration we need and a merciful reprieve from the hay fields—my hands can scarce endure another day of raking.

I confess I was not designed for this agrarian life—I feel it in every blister, in the silence that should be filled with my pen. If Ripley's grand scheme is to succeed, it will not be through my triumph over the soil, but through whatever small value my education might impart to these young minds.
