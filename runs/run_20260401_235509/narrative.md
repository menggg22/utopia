## Apr 1841 (Round 1)

*Spring. Food: 100, Money: $500, Morale: 80%*

### George Ripley

**FARM**

*Reasoning*: It is the first day, and I must show by example that at Brook Farm, even the founder does not shy from honest labor. The fields will not work themselves, and I must demonstrate to our members—especially the scholars and poets—that we are serious about this union of intellectual and manual work.

*Inner thought*: My hands are soft. I know this. By evening they will blister, and I will ache in places I didn't know could ache. But if I retreat to my library while Hawthorne and Dana struggle with the plow, how can I ever ask them to believe in this vision? They came here because I promised something different—not a refuge for thinkers, but a *community*, where everyone shares the burden. I must show them I meant it. Though God knows I'm no farmer. I keep thinking about the property deed, the mortgage, all that money from supporters... it all rests on this working. On *me* making it work.

*Observes*: Sophia watched me put on work clothes this morning with a mixture of encouragement and concern in her eyes. The others seem uncertain—Hawthorne especially looks like he's observing all of us as though we're characters in one of his stories rather than human beings trying to build something real.

*Says aloud*: ""Come—the spring sun is high and the soil is ready. Let us all meet at the north field. There is no task too common for the hands of anyone here, myself least of all. We build this community through work, not words alone.""

*Mood*: Determined | *Satisfaction*: 67/100

### Nathaniel Hawthorne

**TEACH**

*Reasoning*: I am, after all, a man of letters—even if this community seems designed to transform everyone into tillers of soil. The schoolhouse offers refuge, intellectual purpose, and honest wages toward Sophia. Let the others break their hands in the field.

*Inner thought*: Ripley's piety about "work, not words" rings hollow to me. Yes, he labored today—admirable, certainly. But I am not cut from that cloth. My mind is my tool, not these blistered palms. Teaching the children will satisfy both duty and self-preservation. I must earn the $500 I came with into a sum worthy of a marriage, a quiet study, perhaps a house by the sea where I can finally *write* without the endless tramping of boots overhead. Four hours instructing will yield payment; four hours raking hay yields only deeper calluses and soul-death.

*Observes*: Ripley returns from the fields flushed with righteousness and virtue. The others regard him with admiration—as if muddy boots and exhaustion are proof of moral character. No one seems to notice that such enthusiasm often masks desperation. They came here fleeing something, too, I suspect. Even Dana, for all his musical refinement, has that hunger in his eyes. We are all fugitives of a sort, though we wear our flight as noble purpose.

*Says aloud*: ""I shall open the schoolhouse. The children's minds need cultivation as much as the fields do. Let someone else manage the spring planting—I was not promised the life of a common laborer.""

*Mood*: Skeptical | *Satisfaction*: 75/100

### Sophia Ripley

**TEACH**

*Reasoning*: The school is the lifeblood of this community—our only reliable income. I cannot leave the children unattended while George rallies the others to the fields, especially not after Hawthorne's dismissal of manual labor yesterday. If the school falters, all the idealism in the world will not feed us.

*Inner thought*: George's words ring true—we *are* building through work. But someone must tend to the actual edifice of learning, and it cannot be anyone else. I notice already that Hawthorne views common labor as beneath him. How many others will follow? The school keeps us solvent. Without it, this whole experiment collapses within a season. My satisfaction comes from the classroom, from watching minds unfold. That is real. Everything else... I am no longer certain.

*Observes*: George moved through the world with such conviction this morning—sleeves rolled, hands ready. There is something admirable in it, something that moved me when we married. But Hawthorne's refusal to join him was not rebellion; it was indifference, even contempt. That troubles me more than any argument could. If our members do not share the conviction, what are we really bound by?

*Says aloud*: ""I shall be in the schoolhouse. The children are waiting, and their lessons will not teach themselves. George, the fields can wait for my hands—yours and the others' are sufficient. But do not expect all of us to abandon our posts. Some of us have work that cannot be postponed.""

### Charles Dana

*Inner thought*: I see it already. Ripley calls everyone to the soil, but Hawthorne and Sophia have their own work, and they're right. ### John Sullivan Dwight

*Reasoning*: The community's spirit must be nurtured from the first day. Tonight, I shall organize an evening gathering—nothing formal, merely an opportunity for us to join hands in a circle, perhaps with music. The fields can wait; the human soul cannot.

*Inner thought*: *Finally—finally!—I am free to do what I was born to do.* That pulpit was a cage of expectations, but here, in this spring evening, I can feel the shape of my true life taking form. Hawthorne and Sophia speak of duty and lessons; Dana speaks of ideas in print. But I know something they do not yet: a community without music, without beauty, without gathered voices in harmony, is merely a farm with pretentious ideals. Let them toil in the soil. I shall cultivate the heart. My piano—yes, I must see that it is brought up from Boston—will be the foundation of everything here.

*Observes*: George Ripley works with the zealotry of a man trying to prove something to the world; Hawthorne avoids the fields with barely concealed reluctance; Sophia teaches with genuine warmth to the children; Charles Dana's solitude seems purposeful, almost righteous. *Says aloud*: ""My friends, the fields are tended and the school awaits tomorrow. But this evening—shall we gather? I propose we meet at sunset in the Hive's parlor. ## Jun 1841 (Round 2)

*Spring. Food: 83, Money: $525, Morale: 85%*

### George Ripley

**REPAIR The Hive**

*Reasoning*: The house shows wear and weather will worsen the damage. I cannot control whether Hawthorne works the fields or Dana abandons his desk, but I can control this—I can hammer a nail, patch a roof, make something solid with my own hands. Perhaps action speaks louder than exhortation.

*Inner thought*: *I watched them all refuse the fields this morning, and it cut deeper than I care to admit. Hawthorne's words were honest, at least—he never promised transcendence. But I did. I promised that here, the scholar and the laborer would merge into one whole person. Instead, they're splintering again, each retreating to what comes easiest. I am failing already, and it has only been two days. But The Hive—the Hive is something I can repair. My hands are not a farmer's hands, but they can hold a hammer. Let me prove I am not merely a dreamer.*

*Observes*: This morning revealed the shape of things. Hawthorne and Dana and even Sophia—they are all, in their own ways, seeking exemption from the common labor. Only Dwight seems to embrace the manual work without resentment. I notice he is the only one who has not already positioned himself as indispensable elsewhere. The others have built their escapes so quickly. It is clever, in a way. It is also what I feared most.

*Says aloud*: "*I gather tools from the barn, calling out to anyone within earshot:* "I am mending The Hive today. If anyone wishes to help, I welcome it. But I shall not command it. We build this community on choice, not coercion—though I confess I do not yet know if choice alone will hold us together.""

### Nathaniel Hawthorne

**WRITE**

*Reasoning*: I must attempt to recover some portion of my soul, even if the world conspires against it. Yesterday I taught children their lessons; today I shall teach myself what I came here to learn—whether a man can create whilst surrounded by chaos.

*Inner thought*: This is folly, perhaps. I know I shall find no quiet. Already I hear the sound of Ripley's hammer against wood, and Dwight's voice carries through the Hive like an aria of relentless optimism. Yet if I surrender my pen entirely to blisters and ledgers, I may as well have remained in Salem, trapped in obscurity. The irony cuts deep: I came here to *free* myself through means, only to discover that the means themselves become chains. But I shall try. If I do not attempt to write today, when shall I? When the farm is prosperous? When Sophia finally consents to marry a man who has forgotten how to write? Never. The only moment is now, however imperfect.

*Observes*: Dana's absence from the morning work does not go unnoticed—he has claimed his solitude, and I see in it both an indictment and a possibility. Ripley works steadily with his tools, the picture of earnest devotion. Even Dwight's musical invitation last night was calculated, I think, to bind us all together through sentiment. *Says aloud*: ""I shall take the morning in The Nest. I have correspondence to attend to—matters of some importance. ## Aug 1841 (Round 3)

### George Ripley

**REPAIR <The Hive>**

*Reasoning*: I called for help this morning, and though Hawthorne declined, I cannot abandon what I have begun. The main house is the heart of Brook Farm—if it crumbles, what does that say about my vision? I must see this through myself, even if it costs me money and exhausts me further.

*Says aloud*: "*As I hammer a nail, I speak aloud to myself, not quite conscious that I am doing it:* "A man who cannot build with his own hands has no right to ask others to abandon their gifts for soil and stone.
