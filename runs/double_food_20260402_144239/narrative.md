### George Ripley

**FARM**

*Reasoning*: On this first morning, the others will be watching. If I ask them to till soil and milk cows, I must show my own hands are willing—no matter how clumsy they prove. We began this venture to prove that mind and body can work in harmony. I cannot ask for what I will not give.

*Inner thought*: *My back already aches from the ride out here yesterday. I know nothing of farming—my strength has always been in books and arguments, not in the breaking of earth. But Hawthorne is watching from the Hive, and so is Charles Dana. If they see me retreat indoors to my library while they sweat in the fields, the whole experiment collapses before it begins. The intellectuals will do their share, I will ensure it—by example, if necessary. Though God knows my hands will be raw by sundown.*

*Observes*: The others seem tentative this morning—Sophia moves between the buildings with purpose, organizing the kitchen stores, while Hawthorne stands at the fence line, observing the land with that inscrutable expression of his. Dwight is tuning his violin indoors (I heard it this dawn). No one has yet ventured to the fields. They are waiting for me.

*Says aloud*: ""The morning is splendid. Shall we begin? The spring planting will not wait for us to philosophize about it. Come—there is soil to turn near the eastern pasture. We shall work together.""

### Nathaniel Hawthorne

**FARM**

*Reasoning*: Ripley's invitation is direct and the fields await. As a trustee and newly arrived member, I cannot refuse on the first day without undoing what goodwill exists. Besides, I came here to build capital, and capital begins with labor—however bitter that notion.

*Inner thought*: God help me. Already the physical work calls. My hands, so accustomed to pen and paper, will soon be raw. I tell myself this is temporary—a means to an end. Sophia waits in Boston. But even now, scarcely arrived, I sense the crushing irony: I came here seeking peace and money to marry, and instead I find myself in a field under a spring sun, aware that I am watching my own life happen to me rather than living it. Ripley's idealism is contagious in its earnestness, yet I suspect we are all deluding ourselves. Still—the fields must be worked. The money will not materialize otherwise.

*Observes*: Ripley moves with genuine enthusiasm, his sleeves already rolled. The others follow his lead with varying degrees of conviction. I notice Charles Dana watches Ripley with almost religious fervor. Dwight seems content enough. Sophia Ripley smiles at her husband's energy. They all seem to believe we are building something sacred here. I wonder how long that belief will sustain them when their hands blister as mine already threaten to do.

*Says aloud*: ""Very well, Mr. Ripley. I shall join you in the eastern pasture. The soil will not turn itself, and spring waits for no man's philosophical reservations.""

### Sophia Ripley

**TEACH**

*Reasoning*: The school does not run itself, and our students depend upon consistency. While George and Nathaniel turn the soil, I must tend the minds — that is where the true work of this community lives or dies.

*Inner thought*: George works the fields with such poetry in his voice, as if the soil itself listens to his philosophy. But soil is indifferent. Only discipline and sustained effort matter. The school must prosper, must prove something. If it fails, everything else here collapses into pretty talk and broken promises. The children from Cuba, from Philadelphia—their parents trust me. I will not betray that trust with romantic notions about communal farming.

*Observes*: I notice Nathaniel is willing to labor alongside George without the fervent conviction the rest of us carry. There is something honest in that restraint. He writes in his notebook at supper. I wonder what he truly makes of us.

*Says aloud*: ""Good morning, children. We shall begin with Latin composition. Miss Margaret, you have your texts? Attend now—a well-turned phrase requires more discipline than sentiment. That is something this farm must learn, if we are to survive.""

### Charles Dana

**TRADE**

*Inner thought*: Five hundred dollars will not last long with five mouths to feed. I've already done the arithmetic in my head—we're bleeding money on every decision. Ripley's ideals are sound, but ideals do not pay suppliers. Better to think like a merchant today and a philosopher tomorrow. Besides, this work plays to my real skill: moving goods, moving people, making things happen. Already I can see where the financial pressures will come. *Inner thought*: I confess, I cannot bear the thought of endless plowing and manual drudgery. ## Aug 1841 (Round 3)

### George Ripley

**REPAIR <The Hive>**

*Reasoning*: The eastern wall cannot wait—Charles Dana saw the decay this morning, and I cannot ask others to tend what their leader neglects. Perhaps working alongside them in honest repair will answer the questions I see forming in their eyes, particularly Nathaniel's. This is no longer philosophy; this is shelter.

*Inner thought*: I am terrified. Not of the wall—of what its decay represents. If I cannot hold together even a building, how can I hold together this vision? Hawthorne avoided the fields again with his correspondence excuse. I see him writing, always writing, as though the farm itself is merely material for observation rather than commitment. And I am complicit—I wanted him here, wanted his presence to validate this experiment. Now I fear he is the canary in the coal mine: the moment a brilliant mind realizes the dirt doesn't wash from under the fingernails, that principle cannot pay a mortgage, he will flee back to Salem and tell the whole world Brook Farm was a fraud. And he would be right if *I* cannot even fix what is breaking. My hands are no farmer's hands, no carpenter's hands. But they are all I have.

*Observes*: Charles Dana's offer this morning was not casual—it was a test. He stands ready to work, but he watches to see if *I* will commit my body to what I ask of others. Dwight is lost in the music of Latin words while the building cracks. Sophia holds everything steady with her teaching, but even her strength cannot substitute for the men doing what they promised. And Hawthorne—Hawthorne worked in the fields yesterday or the day before, and I watched the effort it cost him, and I felt responsible for his suffering.

*Says aloud*: ""Charles—I heard you this morning about the Hive's wall. Let me work it alongside you. I have neglected the practical too long while preaching necessity. Show me what must be done. The others may think I am unfit for such labor, and they would be right—but fitness is not the measure here. Faithfulness is. Bring the tools.""

**WRITE**

If the farm steals my capacity for that, it has stolen everything I came here to preserve. I write fitfully, guarding this hour like a miser guards coin. *Observes*: I notice that the others have settled into their roles with unsettling speed. Ripley works the walls now alongside Dana, proving his sincerity through calluses. ### Charles Dana

**WRITE**

*Reasoning*: The Harbinger needs content, and my journalism is the foundation of everything I mean to build beyond this place. Even as my eyes burn, I cannot afford to stop writing—not when every piece strengthens both the community's voice and my own prospects.

*Inner thought*: *I tell myself it is duty, but it is ambition.* My eyes ache. The strain from last night's accounts sits behind them like a warning I refuse to read. But if I rest today, I rest tomorrow, and the day after I am just another resident doing farm work and teaching schedules—which is not why I came here. I came to learn, to write, to build something. Hawthorne's despair last night terrified me precisely because I recognized it: the fear that months from now I will have learned nothing except how much labor costs. The Harbinger is my stake in this place. It is also my escape route. I need both.

*Observes*: George Ripley worked the wall with me yesterday—saw something in his face I haven't seen before. Not doubt. Something closer to necessity. He knows what the arithmetic says, I think.
