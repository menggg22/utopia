### George Ripley

**FARM**

*Reasoning*: On this first day, I must demonstrate that a founder does not shrink from physical labor. If I expect our intellectuals to take up the hoe and spade, I must be first in the fields, mud on my boots, my hands blistered alongside theirs.

*Inner thought*: *The soil is heavy and reluctant—my back already aches, and we've barely begun. I am no farmer; my hands are shaped for a pen, not a plow. But this is the whole point, isn't it? We cannot build a community on theory alone. I watch the others work and feel the weight of proving something not just to them, but to myself. What if I fail at this? What if my vision crumbles in the spring mud?*

*Observes*: Hawthorne works steadily but with a distant expression—there is a melancholy in him I recognize from my own ministry days. Sophia moves with quiet competence, correcting my grip on the spade without saying a word. Young Dwight seems genuinely earnest. I notice Dana already calculating something—cost of labor, perhaps, or yield per acre. Good. We need such minds.

*Says aloud*: ""The earth receives what we give it, friends. Let us give it our honest effort. This is not drudgery—it is communion with the land that sustains us, and with one another.""

*Mood*: Determined | *Satisfaction*: 67/100

### Nathaniel Hawthorne

**TEACH**

*Reasoning*: If I am to serve as Trustee of Finance, I must first establish myself as a contributor of value—and my mind remains my only reliable asset. Teaching will generate income toward Sophia without destroying what remains of my hands and spirits.

*Inner thought*: Ripley's words about communion with the earth are pleasant enough, but I did not leave my writing desk to become a farmhand. Let others glory in the soil. I shall instruct the youth and preserve what wit and reason God granted me. Perhaps the schoolhouse will offer some quiet for my own thoughts, if not my pen. The money earned here—that is the true communion I require. Five hundred dollars will not secure a home for Sophia and myself. I must be shrewd.

*Observes*: Ripley himself worked the fields yesterday—genuinely, it appears, not merely orchestrating. There is something almost touching in his faith, though it strikes me as the faith of a man who has never truly wanted for comfort. The others watch him as sheep watch a shepherd. I note that no rules have yet been established; the community operates on trust and goodwill. This will not last.

*Says aloud*: ""I shall undertake the instruction of our students this spring. I trust my background in letters will prove useful to the community's intellectual aims. Shall we establish what subjects require teaching?""

**TEACH**

What does not fail is a well-educated mind—I have seen that in my own life, and I will build it here for others, particularly for girls who are told their minds matter less. The school thrives when I am present. Two absences in six years—that is not carelessness, that is conviction. If the community crumbles, at least the girls I teach will carry something forward. That must be enough.

*Observes*: Hawthorne seems earnest enough, though his hands are unmarked by work—he is already gravitating toward the intellectual work while the rest of us will toil. *Says aloud*: ""Good morning. I trust you all slept well. The morning lesson begins shortly. Our students arrive this week from Salem and Boston—we must show them that Brook Farm offers an education as rigorous as any academy in the city. ## Jun 1841 (Round 2)

*Spring. Food: 83, Money: $535, Morale: 85%*

### George Ripley

**PROPOSE <RULE: All members shall work the fields a minimum of 4 hours weekly, regardless of other duties—no exceptions>**

*Reasoning*: Hawthorne's questions yesterday planted a seed in my chest that will not quiet. I must establish from the beginning that this is not merely talk. If we do not bind ourselves to the common labor now, in these early days when the work is manageable and the morale still high, we shall lose the thread entirely.

*Inner thought*: I am terrified. Dwight's violin was beautiful last night—too beautiful. I saw him lose himself in the playing, and part of me understood the peace of it. But another part saw a man drifting toward the library and away from the soil. And Hawthorne watched it all with those searching eyes, measuring the gap between what we say and what we do. I cannot be that gap. I will not. If I propose this rule and vote for it myself first, it means something. It means I am binding myself to the work I ask of others. It means I am not merely preaching.

*Observes*: Sophia was already up before dawn, preparing the morning lesson and the larder—she needs no rule to remind her of duty. Charles asked thoughtful questions about curriculum balance. Nathaniel observed everything, saying little. John was still humming fragments of last night's music as he crossed the yard. That hum troubles me more than I can articulate.

*Says aloud*: ""Friends, before the day scatters us into our labors, I must speak plainly. We are a community built on a single principle: that intellectual and physical work are not opposed, but woven together. I propose, therefore, that we establish this rule: each of us shall give no fewer than four hours weekly to the fields and gardens. This applies to all equally—to myself as much as anyone. I shall demonstrate this rule first. We shall vote this evening. Brook Farm must prove itself in the soil, or it proves itself nowhere.""

*Mood*: Resolute | *Satisfaction*: 65/100

### Nathaniel Hawthorne

**WRITE**

*Inner thought*: I lie to myself. There is no quiet here. Ripley's voice carries through the Hive before dawn. The roosters begin at first light. And yet—if I do not write, I do not know who I am. I came here for Sophia, yes, for money to marry her. But what good is money if I have surrendered the thing that makes the money possible? I must try. Even if these pages are scratched in desperation between dawn and the first call to work, they must exist. Yesterday my hands bled from raking hay. Today, at least, they will hold a pen.

*Observes*: Charles Dana carries himself with an ease I do not possess—as though he has already made peace with some adjustment of his former life. He speaks of languages as "doors, not drills," and I wonder if he believes that, or merely says it. John Sullivan Dwight seems genuinely moved by his music, genuinely at home in the evenings. *Says aloud*: "*To myself, at my desk, turning to face the eastern window:* "Let us see if solitude can be found in the early hours. Or if, failing that, a man can write through the noise of the world anyway.""
